essays

My third year at Oxford was structured almost entirely around tutorials. Once a week, I'd sit one-to-one with a professor who had read whatever essay I'd written, and we'd spend an hour pulling it apart together. Essays were the assessment, and they were also the way both of us could see whether I'd actually understood a topic. As a non-native English speaker, the writing side of this was disproportionately hard — every essay was an exercise in thinking and translating at the same time, and most of them, especially the earlier ones, are a record of struggling more than anything else.

What follows is a small archive of essays from that year that I still like. Less because the writing is polished than because the questions still feel worth chewing on. Comments are open on each page — corrections, disagreements, and pointers to better references are all welcome.

Is cancer "genetic"?

Advanced Cell · 2024

Whether cancer is heritable or environmental — and why the honest answer is "both." Driver mutations in Rb, KRAS, and XPV on one side; AFB1 adducts at TP53 codon 249 in sporadic liver cancer on the other.

Senescence

Advanced Cell · 2024

On the difference between aging and cellular senescence — what stops cells dividing, why our bodies sometimes want them stopped, and what the new senotherapeutic drugs are actually doing.

Organelle ecology: compartments without membranes

Advanced Cell · 2024

How cells organise themselves with phase-separated droplets instead of lipid bilayers. Nucleoli, P-granules, and pyrenoids as case studies in biology getting work done without a wall around it.

Genomic imprinting in plants

Genomes · 2024

Why some genes only get read from one parent. A story about DNA methylation and DEMETER glycosylases — and, underneath the mechanism, an evolutionary tug-of-war between maternal and paternal interests in how much the offspring eats.
