Genomic imprinting in plants: what is it, and how did it evolve?

Genomes tutorial · Oxford · 2024

introduction

In diploid and polyploid organisms, the majority of genes are typically expressed from both maternal and paternal alleles. However, in some species — in insects, mammals, and angiosperms — we may observe monoallelic preferential expression, which depends on whether the gene is inherited from the mother or from the father. This phenomenon is called genomic imprinting and it relies on epigenetic marks — "imprints" — that are asymmetrically distributed on maternal and paternal chromosomes prior to fertilisation event, so when inherited these imprints govern differential expression of genes in the offspring specific tissues. For example, in the well-studied plant Arabidopsis thaliana, a relatively small number of imprinted genes have been identified, and these are primarily expressed in the endosperm. This is in contrast to the situation in mammals, where imprinting can affect the expression of genes in many different tissues. Therefore, in angiosperms generally, imprinting affects a specific subset of genes, and these are implicated in growth regulation, nutrient transfer, development, and resource allocation to the offspring.

In angiosperms, the mechanism and timing of imprinting events is unique due to the nature of reproductive cycle: they have independently evolved double fertilization and the resource allocation structure — endosperm. In endosperm we tend to predominantly observe imprinting. The endosperm develops after fertilization of the diploid central cell in the embryo sac with the haploid sperm cell from the pollen. Central cell develops from the fusion of two polar nuclei that were formed through three consecutive cycles of mitotic division from the megaspore in the gametophyte (embryo sac). Therefore, it carries the same but doubled DNA which is also epigenetically distinct from the egg cell. Notably, after fertilization, eukaryotic zygote generally undergoes chromatin remodeling; however, unlike in mammals, the endosperm and embryo genome is not completely reorganized, it rather undergoes slight local chromatin changes, still leaving the inherited methylation patterns. These methylation pattern from maternal and paternal chromosomes are asymmetrical, therefore result in the preferential expression of the alleles of specific origin.

Following an intricate nature of genomic imprinting, in this essay, I will discuss the mechanisms of origin-specific preferential expression of alleles and talk about the evolutionary drivers and consequences of the imprinting evolution.

mechanism of epigenetic modifications

Imprinting is controlled by epigenetic mechanisms, which are changes that affect gene activity without altering the DNA sequence. These modifications include DNA methylation and histone modification, which essentially tag the DNA to regulate gene expression. The regions that differ in their methylation patterns in maternal and paternal genomes are called differentially methylated regions (DMR). Genes in DMRs that are predominantly expressed from the maternally inherited allele are referred to as maternally expressed genes (MEGs), and vice a versa for the paternally expressed genes (PEG).

maternal hypomethylation induces expression of MEGs

It has been observed that in the endosperm of flowering plants, there is a genome-wide hypomethylation of transposon and repeat sequences. Moreover, in endosperm of Arabidopsis, rice, and maize, hypomethylation is also established in the specific sites of MEGs. It has been later shown that demethylation, indeed, occurs primarily in the maternal genome of the central cell within the female gametophyte before fertilisation. The key enzymes, DNA glycosylases — DEMETER (DME) and REPRESSOR OF SILENCING 1 (ROS1) [mainly in rice] are responsible for removing methyl groups from 5-methylcytosines through the base excision repair mechanism in the specific regions, including those associated with imprinted genes.

Though DME and ROS1 are associated with the gene imprinting, what is their primary role in the flowering plants? These glycosylases function specifically in the central cell of the female gametophyte and mainly demethylate transposable elements (TEs) and small repetitive sequences in the targeted regions. McDonald et al. (2005) and Gehring et al. (2009) proposed that this mechanism evolved as a strategic defence against TEs in the plant gametes hence for protection of the genetic integrity in the embryo. Demethylation in the central cell can lead to the transcriptional activation of TEs, which in turn produces small interfering RNAs (siRNAs). They are essential for initiating DNA methylation through the RNA-directed DNA methylation (RdDM) pathway, which uses siRNAs as guides to target the DNA methylation machinery to homologous sequences. These siRNAs though do not silence genes in the central cell, but are speculated to move to the egg cell, where they direct the silencing of TEs and therefore act for protection of the embryo genome. Interestingly, in the central cell, the flanking regions of the imprinted genes are often enriched with TEs, therefore DME-mediated demethylation of flanking regions has an impact on the expression of the genes themselves. As a result, it was proposed that imprinted gene expression exists as a by-product of the DME activity in defence against TEs in the egg cell. The great examples of MEGs in Arabidopsis are the members of the homeodomain-leucine zipper (HD-ZIP) family of transcription factors — HDG8 and HDG9 — have been identified through their hypomethylated state and endosperm-specific expression patterns. This pattern aligns with the notion that demethylation of maternal alleles can result in a bias towards maternal expression in the endosperm.

In conclusion, after fertilization, the demethylated maternal genome in the central cell contributes to the development of the endosperm, where gene expression patterns are established based on the earlier demethylation events. This process ensures the proper expression of imprinted genes in the endosperm, critical for seed development in flowering plants.

asymmetry between DME in the vegetative cell and MET1 in the sperm cell

In contrast to the maternal chromosomes, paternal ones do not exhibit demethylation profiles as two sperms travelling down the pollen tube could perform interchangeable functions depending on whether they fertilise an egg cell or a central cell. However, to confer MEGs, we need asymmetry in the allele methylation pattern — both demethylation of the imprinted gene on the maternal allele and conserved methylation of the same gene on the paternal allele. Therefore, the same way as in the central cell, in the vegetative male gamete, DME and ROS1 demethylates TEs at the specific genes, which seems to be crucial for the pollen tube's journey towards the ovule. Moreover, siRNA, transcribed from demethylated TEs, then travels to both of the neighbouring sperm cells to methylate and hence silence the same MEGs on the paternal allele through RdDM. Besides methylation of the paternal alleles of some MEGs, the activity of DNA METHYLTRANSFERASE 1 (MET1) is required for the maintenance of CG and CHG methylation levels in sperm cells, respectively, ensuring epigenetic inheritance.

Together, the different dynamics of DNA methylation pathways in male and female gametes results in the inheritance of a locally demethylated maternal genome and a hypermethylated paternal genome in the endosperm. As a consequence, DMRs can be defined, where the maternal allele is often hypomethylated, especially in the context of DMRs associated with imprinted genes. Multiple studies have previously demonstrated that MEGs are often associated with DMRs where the paternal allele has higher methylation than the maternal allele: around 28–54% of MEGs are associated with at least one DMR across different species. Interestingly, inheritance of a demethylated maternal genome in the endosperm is observed in all plants analysed thus far, suggesting that the process of central cell demethylation is conserved across different species.

histone modifications as a secondary imprint regulating PEGs

Other mechanism involved in the origin-specific expression specifically implicates Polycomb group (PcG) proteins and the repressive complexes (PRC) they form to modify histones, promote chromatin condensation and inhibit transcription. Following hypomethylation of TE-rich flanking regions in the maternal genome, the PRC, namely PRC2, recognises transposons associated with DME-mediated demethylation. Demethylated TEs at the PEG areas of maternal chromosomes are then recognised by PRC2 and tagged with trimethyl mark on the Lys27 of histone H3. This binding leads to the silencing of genes on the maternal chromosome, while the paternal allele is active, thus defining a PEG. Therefore, to be supressed, the gene generally must be initially demethylated — this is supported by the fact that deposition of H3K27me3 is anticorrelated with the presence of DNA methylation (Roudier et al. 2011; Deleris et al. 2012). The histone modification is therefore considered as the secondary imprint that requires the presence of the primary.

In Arabidopsis species, a significant number of PEGs are marked with maternal H3K27me3, with varying proportions in A. lyrata and A. thaliana (36% in A. lyrata and 55% in A. thaliana). One example of such pattern of imprinting is that of the MADS-box transcription factor PHERES 1 (PHE1), which is flanked by 3′ repetitive sequences. In the central cell, sequences near PHE1 undergo demethylation by DME, enabling H3K27me3 deposition on maternal alleles, thus silencing them. Conversely, the paternal allele of PHE1, due to DNA methylation, resists H3K27me3 deposition, keeping these alleles active in the endosperm. This dynamic between DME-mediated DNA demethylation and PRC2-driven histone modification is essential for regulating genomic imprinting and gene expression in flowering plants.

evolutionary origin and significance

The evolutionary origin and significance of genomic imprinting in plants, insects, and mammals highlight its convergent evolution and its potential role in reproductive strategies.

The kinship or parental conflict theory, particularly relevant in these organisms, suggests that imprinting evolved due to conflict between maternal and paternal interests in the level of resources an embryo obtains from the mother. Imprinting especially enables divergence in maternal and paternal interests in species where females can have offspring from multiple males. Therefore, in this case, mothers aim for equal resource allocation to offspring, while fathers benefit from maximizing the resources received by their progeny, leading to a selection for parentally biased gene expression.

In flowering plants and therian mammals, imprinting evolved alongside transient nutritive tissues like the placenta and endosperm. In plants, imprinting mainly occurs in the endosperm, influencing resource allocation from the mother plant to the offspring. PEGs often enhance growth and resource uptake, MEGs typically limit growth and equals the nutrient distribution. This mechanism balances the interests of both parental genomes during seed development.

In contrast, in insects, the relationship between resource allocation and imprinting doesn't fully align with this theory. Here, imprinting might be an evolutionary by-product of regulatory circuits associated with transposable elements. However, in haplodiploid social insects, where females develop from fertilized eggs and males from unfertilized eggs, the kinship theory of imprinting is extended to encompass conflicts over treatment of relatives to which matrigenes (genes inherited from the mother) and patrigenes (genes inherited from the father) are differently related. The theory posits that in such societies, genomic imprinting could be a response to social and reproductive dynamics, where genes are differentially expressed based on their parental origin and therefore determines the social role of an offspring in a colony. Therefore, this differential expression could influence behaviours and decisions that impact the colony's overall fitness, such as division of labour, reproductive roles, and the raising of offspring. Overall, other theories such as dosage, coadaptation, evolution under relaxed selection hypotheses exist — leaving floor for speculations about possible origin and mechanism of genomic imprinting. While the exact evolutionary pathways are still debated, it's clear that imprinting is particularly significant in organisms with internal gestation and placental development, or in seed endosperm development in flowering plants.

conclusion

Genomic imprinting in plants is a complex and fascinating phenomenon, with profound implications for our understanding of genetics, development, and evolution. In plants, DNA methylation is a key mechanism in establishing and maintaining imprinting. Methylation patterns set in the gametes or early after fertilization can lead to the silencing of one parental allele. Histone modifications also play a role in imprinting, affecting chromatin structure and gene accessibility. These different mechanisms allow for the interplay between maternal and paternal genomes in the endosperm. Therefore, imprinting impacts resource allocation and development of the plant and plays a critical role in the plant reproduction. The evolutionary origins and significance of this phenomenon is usually discussed in the context of the kinship or parental conflict theory. This theory provides a compelling explanation for the evolution of imprinting in plants and mammals, suggesting it arose as a strategy to resolve conflicts between maternal and paternal interests in resource allocation to offspring. In conclusion, genomic imprinting in plants represents a critical intersection of genetics, development, and evolutionary biology. It highlights the adaptive significance of epigenetic mechanisms in shaping developmental processes and evolutionary strategies.
