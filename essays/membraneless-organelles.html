Organelle ecology: how functional compartmentalisation can occur without membrane-bound organelles

Advanced Cell tutorial · Oxford · 2024

It has long been though that cellular compartmentalisation could only be achieved through formation of membrane-bound organelles and vesicles. However, the discovery of membrane-less organelles has significantly challenged this membrane-centric worldview. These organelles are also called biomolecular condensates, which are formed by the process of liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS). Inside the cell they function as inert reservoirs of important molecular components segregating these elements from the rest of the cellular milieu.

The molecular basis of LLPS involves the spontaneous segregation of specific biomolecules into concentrated phases that coexist with a less concentrated, surrounding environment. More specifically, phase separation occurs if the local viscosity prevents mixing with other regions, much like oil segregating into droplets in water. Drivers for phase separation involve weak multivalent dynamic interactions that could be assumed my proteins and nucleic acids forming weak and reversible bonds. For example, many proteins involved in LLPS contain intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs). These regions often composed of polar and charged amino acids, like phenylalanine and tyrosine, which allow them to flexibly interact with multiple partners and participate in the formation of condensates, without assuming fixed three-dimensional structure. On the other hand, repeated modular domains, such as SUMO and SIM, that can engage in multiple simultaneous interactions, can also facilitate phase transition. Bifunctional proteins are a well-studied example of proteins with both modular nature and often distinct IDR. In bifunctional protein, the tethering domain binds to a specific nucleic sequence or a protein mark, whereas multiple binding modules and IDRs promote formation of a condensate. As the binding interactions increase there is a phase transition to form a condensate domain. Overall, any multivalent interaction involving formation of weak and reversible bonds allows for compartmentalisation and concentration of protein and RNA molecules at specific loci without membrane barriers.

In this essay, I will examine the mechanisms that drive assembly of the membrane-less organelles and the role they play in cellular physiology to argue that functional compartmentalisation could be achieved without the traditional confines of lipid bilayer membranes.

organisation of the nucleolus

One of the striking examples of the membrane-less self-organising compartments are nucleoli. These are large nuclear sub-compartments, whose primary function is ribosome biogenesis. They are typically formed around nucleolar organiser regions (NORs) on chromosomes that code for ribosomal RNA. The ribosomal genes are organised in a tandem manner, each gene is transcribed by the RNA polymerase I (RNAPI). In the rapidly growing cell, the RNA synthesis is prioritised and therefore, rRNA can make up to 80% of total synthesised RNA, causing NOR be associated with increased concertation of RNA. When stained at the EM level, each nucleolus shows three distinct subcompartments with different density: a fibrillar center (FC), the dense fibrillar component (DFC) and the granular component (GC). Even though this tri-partite structure is not bounded by the membrane, it is visible at the EM and could be physically isolated as a discrete structure coated with heterochromatin. How do nucleoli maintain such a segregated structure without a distinct membrane?

Discrete structures are formed and maintained due to the differing densities of aggregates forming throughout ribosomal assembly. In higher eukaryotes, the regions containing NORs and RNAPI forms a fibrillar center, there rRNA is transcribed as a single strand of 45S pre-rRNA. As nascent transcript emerges it becomes instantly bound by the RNA-binding domain (RBD) of the fibrillarin proteins (FBLs). FBL is a bifunctional protein; besides RBD it contains intrinsically disordered Gly-Arg-rich domain, which allows the protein to self-segregate. Large concentrations of FBL-bound pre-rRNA lead to phase separation from FC and establishment of DFC phase. Such physical phase separation promotes native sorting of the nascent transcript from the FC–DFC interface to the DFC and allows for initial pre-rRNA processing. At the DFC pre-rRNA could then be easily spliced to form 28S, 18S and 5.8S rRNAs.

These pre-rRNA species are further processed by specialized protein-processing complexes including small nucleolar RNPs (snoRNPs) leading to their maturation. Mature 28S, 18S, and 5.8S rRNAs are assembled with ribosomal proteins to form the small and large subunits. Dissociation of RNA from fibrillarins and their interaction with larger oligomerising proteins, such as nucleophosmin NPM1, causes another liquid-liquid phase separation and formation of GC phase. As rRNA assembles with ribosomal proteins into ribosomal subunits, the number of contacts with nucleophosmin decreases. This leads to the lower solubility in the granular component and 'selective exclusion' of fully processed and assembled subunits. Therefore, in this case, phase-phase separation allows to sort 40S and 80S subunits and initiate the export from the nucleus through the nuclear pore complexes and give rise to active ribosomes in the cytoplasm.

As a result, nucleoli form in a 'sequential' fashion, starting with the fibrillar centre (FC), followed by the dense fibrillar component (DFC) and finishing with the granular component (GC). rRNA is radially expelled as it is transcribed, processed, modified and folded with proteins into ribosomal subunits. This entire nucleolar substructure that plays a crucial role in ribosome biogenesis is coordinated thoroughly through phase-phase separation without requirement for a membrane.

p-granules

Apart from facilitating membrane-less compartmentalisation, phase-phase separation in biological systems can help in guarding developmental processes. In eukaryotic organisms such as C. elegans, P-granules, formed through LLPS, are involved in marking germ line cells and driving patterning during early embryonic development. By segregating germ line from somatic cells, P-granules participate in the preservation and expression of genetic material vital for future generations.

Following fertilization, sperm entry initiates cytoskeletal remodelling that localizes PAR-2 at the embryo's posterior pole, triggering acto-myosin contractions. These contractions create a cortical flow that spreads the PAR-2 domain, establishing a bipolar gradient with its antagonist, PAR-3. This gradient, maintained by a Cdc42 – PAR-6 – PKC complex at the anterior pole, is crucial for cellular polarity. Along with polarisation and pro-nuclear migration, MEG protein assembles at the posterior pole. MEG contains several multimerization domains and IDRs that facilitate phase-phase separation. MEG-3 condensates enrich selected germline-associated mRNAs at the posterior cytoplasm, where they recruit and stabilize P-granule component (PGL). More specifically, PGL-1 contains repeating sequences of arginine (R) and glycine (G) in a RGG domain that is known to tightly bind RNA and control its expression. Following the interaction between MEG, PGL and maternal mRNA, the dense P-granules are formed with liquid phase constituted by PGL and supporting gel-like phase made with IDRs in MEG-3. As a result of their asymmetric distribution, the inheritance of P-granules is unequal during cell divisions. Such separation of functional genes allows for asymmetric cell division and further patterning of the embryo.

pyrenoid

Pyrenoids form part of the carbon concentrating mechanisms (CCM) in many algae. It concentrates Rubisco at the higher levels of the CO₂, however, the structure doesn't have a membrane, it is surrounded by starch membranes. Pyrenoid tubules deliver CO₂ to the pyrenoid matrix. These are major controls of flux of CO₂. There is evidence in Chlamydomonas that the pyrenoid forms by LLPS. Rubisco packing consistent with liquid-like organisation not crystalline array.

  • The pyrenoid is divided typically at the end of chloroplast division.
  • Often splits into two droplets.
  • Occasionally one daughter does not inherit any of the mother pyrenoid.
  • In these cases, the pyrenoid forms de novo from aggregation of new droplets.

Cyanobacterial carboxysomes also have similar LLPS components. Rubisco in cyanobacteria and algae interacts with multi-valent proteins to form condensates: Essential Pyrenoid Component 1 (EPYC1) in pyrenoids; Carbon Concentrating Mechanism M (CcmM) in carboxysomes. Both have intrinsically disordered domains and form LLPS in vitro. EPYC1 and algal-Rubisco also form LLPS in heterologous expression in higher plants.
