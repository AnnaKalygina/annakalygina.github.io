Is cancer "genetic"?

Advanced Cell tutorial · Oxford · 2024

introduction

According to the Cancer Research UK, the estimated lifetime risk of being diagnosed with cancer is 50% in males and 45% in females, which comprises a strikingly large proportion of people suffering from different sort of cancers in a human population. Cancer is categorised by the uncontrolled growth and division of the cells in the body. These cells may form tumours, evade different tissues and disrupt their physiological function. Cancer cells are phenotypically distinct from other normal cells: unlike primary cells, they breach the Hayflick limit and extend their lifespan by avoiding telomere erosion, evading apoptosis, and sustaining constant growth with self-produced signals. In addition to being able to ignore humoral and intracellular anti-growth signals, cancer cells can promote and sustain angiogenesis, and metastasize to tissues different from their site of origin. What could lead cell to such phenotypical diversion?

The underlying reasons for this diversion are genetic changes: cancer typically originates from a single mutated cell, making it clonal in nature. This is evident from the identical or common mutations found in the cells of tumours. Generally, more than one somatic driver mutation is required to significantly disrupt the normal cell cycle and form a tumour, although there are some examples of tumours caused by a single mutation. On average, cancer cells have a higher number of somatic mutations than normal cells, and some cancers require more than 100 mutations per 10⁶ bases to develop. This data illustrates a strong association of cancer initiation with the genetic changes. Though most of the mutations arise sporadically, in some cases, individual inherits genetic markers that increase the risk of molecular changes which lead to mutagenesis. Therefore, in this essay I will explore the role of genetic changes in cancer, the heritability of cancer, and the interaction between environmental factors and genetic factors in tumour development. I will consider examples of hepatobiliary cancers, that tend to develop sporadically and are associated with exogenous cues, and familial colon cancer, that exhibits a strong heritable component. This comparison will allow me to argue that while not all cancers are heritable, genetic changes play an important role in cancer development, and understanding the genetic basis of cancer is critical for identifying individuals and families at higher risk for developing the disease.

driver mutations and mutational signatures

Genetic changes that disrupt regulatory circuits are the key in development of tumours. Though most of the somatic mutations are neutral, changes in the driver genes are strongly correlated with the switch to abnormal cell cycle. The driver genes are mainly responsible for the regulation of cell cycle and differentiation, suppression of growth and proliferation, and damage repair. First type of driver genes, tumour suppressors are involved in regulating cell growth and division, and mutations in these genes can lead to the uncontrolled proliferation of cells, a hallmark of cancer. For instance, a well-studied retinoblastoma tumour suppressor protein (Rb) is a key regulator of the cell cycle. It functions by binding to and inhibiting the activity of transcription factors known as E2F proteins, which regulate the genes involved in DNA replication and cell proliferation (e.g. cyclin E). In early G1 phase, when Rb is phosphorylated by cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs), presumably cyclin D and E, it releases E2F, allowing for the progression of the cell cycle and further division. If Rb remains unmethylated after the restriction point, the further cell division cycle is sequestrated, the entry to the S-phase and cell growth are blocked (C. Giacinti, Oncogene, 2006). When the gene coding for Rb is mutated and no longer functional, the mitotic control in the cell becomes disrupted, and the cell proliferation is promoted (E. Hernando, Nature, 2004). Functional inactivation of RB through chromosomal mutations is one of the principal reasons for retinoblastoma tumour development. In conclusion, the mutation in tumour suppressor genes, such as Rb, leads to the loss of functions in cell cycle regulators and so can lead to a malignant phenotype. This uncontrolled expression of transcription factors promotes deregulation and increases risks of consecutive mutations in genes associated with cell proliferation, as a result leads to the positive feedback loop formation in cell growth and development.

Oncogenes, on the other hand, promote cell growth and division, and mutations in these genes can cause them to become overactive, leading to the development of cancer. For example, KRAS is an important proto-oncogene that encodes a small protein that binds GTP/GDP and regulate cellular response to many extracellular stimuli (D. Denu, J Med Life, 2014). KRAS is involved in signalling pathways that are initiated by the binding of growth factor receptors. When activated, KRAS binds GTP and activates downstream effectors that promote cell growth, cell transformation, angiogenesis, migration, and differentiation. Over 20 downstream effectors are known to be activated by KRAS. Specifically, KRAS mutations are thought to promote tumour growth by activating the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathway, which is involved in the regulation of cell proliferation, differentiation, and survival. In addition, KRAS mutations can also activate the phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K) pathway, which is involved in the regulation of cell metabolism, growth, and survival. The activation of these pathways can lead to the uncontrolled proliferation of cells and the development of tumours. In conclusion, mutations in oncogenes, such as KRAS or any RAS family protein coding genes, are associated with uncontrolled proliferation and increase risk of cancer.

Finally, genes involved in DNA repair are responsible for repairing any damage to the DNA that occurs during cell division, and mutations in these genes can lead to an accumulation of mutations in the genome, which can contribute to the development and progression of cancer. For example, the familial cancer syndrome, Xeroderma pigmentosum, arises as a result of defects in DNA repair (A.R. Lehmann, Orphanet Journal of Rare Diseases, 2011). This condition manifests itself in a form of hypersensitivity to the UV and is inherited with an autosomal recessive allele. High risks of mutation lead to the generally early onset of the skin cancer, so the XP patients rarely survive to middle age. In healthy individuals, the nucleotide excision and repair (NER) pathway is responsible for detecting and repairing DNA damage caused by environmental factors, such as UV radiation. The pathway consists of several steps, including damage recognition, unwinding of the DNA helix, excision of the damaged DNA strand, and resynthesis of the DNA using the undamaged strand as a template. The most common defect caused by the UV radiation is a formation of a thymine dimer, and in we have a gene, XPV, that encodes a DNA polymerase eta capable of error-free copying of thymidine dimers. In patients with Xeroderma pigmentosum, mutations in one or more of the genes involved in the NER pathway, such as XPV, results in defects in this process. As a result, DNA damage caused by UV radiation and other environmental factors goes unrepaired, leading to the accumulation of mutations and an increased risk of developing skin cancer.

cancers associated with external factors

Besides all listed genetic changes associated with cancer development, there is a vast number of other types of mutations and changes that can result in similar uncontrolled cell proliferation, growth and tumour behaviour. For example, epigenetic changes caused by environmental factors, such as exposure to carcinogens or changes in diet, and can occur throughout an individual's lifetime. Some of the epigenetic changes strongly correlate with risks of tumour development. One of the most well-known types of epigenetic changes is DNA methylation: hypermethylation of tumour suppressor genes caused by exogenous cues can lead to their inactivation and the development of cancer. Therefore, the consideration of the combination of environmental factors and mutations in the driving genes is much more effective than solely relying on the heritability component in finding the reasons of cancer development.

Though there are many examples of cancers that bear a strong heritable competent and are associated with mutations in particular genes, multiple tumours develop sporadically and could be strongly associated with exposure to some external factors rather than defect genes inherited. Cancers that develop in liver, bile glands and ducts, are generally called hepatocellular or hepatobiliary cancers (HBCs) and are predominantly sporadic. Majority of mutational signatures is strongly associated with exposure to certain risk factors, including alcohol consumption, aflatoxin contamination of food, chronic viral hepatitis B and C infection, metabolic diseases such as non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), and exposure to environmental toxins or pollutants. Aflatoxin is a potential genotoxin and the chronic dietary exposure to it is strongly associated with an increased risk of hepatocellular cancer development. Aflotoxin B1 (AFB1) is the most common carcinogenic toxin produced by A. flavus, but is itself not directly genotoxic. As ingested, AFB1 does not interact itself with other cellular macromolecules such as DNA, RNA or proteins. However, in the liver, AFB1 is detoxified by the cytochrome p450 enzymes. Following that, the ingestion of aflatoxin is associated with upregulated expression of cytochromes, such as CYP3A4 and CYP3A5, and constant exposure to it may lead to depletion of detoxifying enzymes. Sometimes liver cytochromes convert AFB1 to genotoxic oxidised derivatives, called 8,9-endo-epoxides and 8,9-exo-epoxides. AFB1-epoxide then intercalates into DNA, generating DNA adducts, such as the AFB1-N7-dG or AFB1-Fapy-dG adducts. This incorporation affects the efficiency of replicative and proofreading polymerases and hence promotes mutagenesis. Specifically, it's been reported that pol κ and pol η when bypassing these adducts show sufficient preference in generating the G to T and G to A mutations (A.K. McCullough et al., Elsevier, 2019). According to the analysis of mutational signatures in hepatobiliary tumour genomes, AFB1-adducts can increase the rate of mutagenesis, leading to G:C>T:A alterations and damaging guanine on the untranscribed strand (Catalogue of Somatic Mutations in Cancers). For instance, it has been reported that AFB1-associated G to T transverions are prevalent in the gene coding for p53, TP53, specifically at codon 249 (ACGG to AGT). The distortion in p53 gene results in erroneous regulation of a cell cycle and may eventually lead to cancer development. In conclusion, exposure to aflotoxin can generate genotoxic AFB1-epoxides, they circulate in a blood and intercalate with DNA to form AFB1 adducts (E. Zhuravleva, Nature, 2022). The concentration of aflotoxin derivatives are elevated in patients with hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), and therefore the some dietary factor may account for the increased risk of developing cancer. The treatment of hepatobiliary cancers associated with aflatoxin exposure depends on the stage and severity of the cancer. Treatment options may include surgery, radiation therapy, chemotherapy, and targeted therapy. In some cases, a liver transplant may be necessary. However, prevention is key in reducing the risk of developing these types of cancers. This can be achieved through measures such as reducing exposure to aflatoxin in food, maintaining a healthy diet and lifestyle, and getting vaccinated against hepatitis B and C. Aflatoxin exposure can also be reduced through the use of agricultural practices such as proper storage and drying of crops, and the use of biological control agents to limit fungal growth.

conclusion

Defective regulatory circuits lead to formation of tumours, these circuits could be disrupted by either initial genetic predisposure, environmental factors, such as viruses, exposure to the exogenous toxins, or both. Genetic changes play a key role in development of tumours: many sporadic cancers arise from abnormal growth through the activation of oncogenes (KRAS), the inactivation of tumour suppressor genes (Rb), defects in DNA repair genes (mismatch repair or NER genes), and epigenetic regulation of expression patterns. In many ways these genetic changes and defects in genes associated with tumour development could be inherited, however, many appear as a result of accumulation of somatic mutations through the lifetime. These genetic changes can enable cells to divide uncontrollably, resist cell death, and invade nearby tissues, ultimately leading to the formation of a tumour. Understanding the genetic and epigenetic changes that contribute to cancer development is critical for the development of new diagnostic tools and therapies. By identifying the genetic and epigenetic alterations that are specific to different types of cancer, researchers can develop targeted therapies that are tailored to the specific molecular characteristics of a patient's tumour. This approach, known as precision medicine, has the potential to improve patient outcomes and reduce the toxicity of cancer treatments.
